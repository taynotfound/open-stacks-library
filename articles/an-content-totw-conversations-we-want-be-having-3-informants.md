There has been chatter about the situation with Prairieland inmate Maegan Morris, who, if I understand these details correctly, initially lied to the police, then was tortured, then cooperated with the police, then stopped cooperating, and finally was sentenced with the same punitive terms as those who did not cooperate at all. 

Others here have already addressed some of the issues raised (see last week's totw conversation), and the conversations specific to Morris are ongoing -- but this topic is less interested in any individual and more concerned with the following... 

1. How it's easy to be consumed by and give lots of attention to controversial situations, which means that the more obvious and clear collaborators, ironically, get less notice, and find it easier to re-enter our spaces.

2. Our assessments of what makes people break, or not, seems confused to me. Sleep deprivation, fear, lies, manipulation, these are things few of us have had to work through (at least not in combination). Training ourselves to deal with those things seems much more reality-based as a response to finding out that people have informed, rather than some blanket, generalized, and simplistic ostracization/violence. For example: I have long pondered the idea of a training program that bangs on doors in the middle of the night, pulls people from their beds, puts them under bright lights, and yells questions at them, as a trial-by-fire kind of process. This can teach people under what circumstances they will break, or can encourage tactics that allow them not to break. I can't imagine actually doing such role play, but I think it could be helpful, in an exigent sadist kind of way.

I think that we (very much including myself) have let us down, by not having more structured, regular, and challenging discussions about a) what it means (physically and emotionally) to be arrested, b) once arrested, how to resist (physically and emotionally), and c) stories from successful AND unsuccessful resistors. This is something that we can rectify in every bookfair, every @ conference, and in any political grouping we find ourselves in, since it is not an anarchist-specific concern.

So the questions:
I. What would you do to better the conversations we have about informants, and informants of various kinds? (Informants will have differing levels of collaboration, over varied periods of time, and differing reactions to having collaborated. How and when does that matter?)

II. What have you seen, read, heard, participated in that has treated the topic with depth and nuance? What were you the most surprised by? Corollary: What have you seen, read, heard, participated in that treated the topic in ways that you wish had been different? Why? What could you have done to make it better?

III. What is your assessment of what changes someone's behavior? What are you basing that assessment on? (Deterrence, which is what most people are claiming as rationale for blanket punishment of collaborators, is notoriously ineffective at changing people's behavior. Just ask the folks who fight the death penalty. What other ideas do we have?)

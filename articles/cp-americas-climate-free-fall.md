Climate tipping points are critical thresholds that trigger unstoppable shifts to another state. The global climate system is full of teetering tipping points that collectively threaten the climate system with radical change. But it’s the change itself, the transition to a tipping point, that is most chaotic. This is climate free fall in America today, dangerously exposing the country to risks overlooked by policy decision-makers.

Climate free fall has been identified by Anders Kevernann, Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research, Germany, Professor of the Dynamics of the Climate System at Institute for Physics and Astrophysics: “The planet is heading for climate free fall, a period where increasingly erratic weather will stress global supply chains, spike insurance losses, and undermine the financial stability modern economies depend on. The greatest economic risks lie not at the endpoint of climate change, but in how we get there.” (_Climate Free Fall: Scientists Warn of Hidden Economic Crisis, Earth’s Systems Become Dangerously Unstable_, Earth Climate, July 18, 2026)

**America’s Climate Free Fall**

America is already experiencing a chaotic climate system that’s effectively climate free fall.

As of July 14, 2026, 46.47% of the Lower States are in drought. (Source: Drought.gov and National Centers for Environmental Information). Drought in the Lower States is an astonishing 40% worse than the ~33% drought index for the world which itself is triple the global drought index of 10-12% in the year 2000.

Nearly one-third (1/3 rd) of the planet is afflicted with drought as climate change-driven heat pulls moisture from soils and plants at unprecedented rates.

Flash floods- The National Weather Service issued double the number of flash flood warnings, totaling 3,600 in 2025, versus the year 2000: “The dramatic escalation in flash flooding over the last quarter-century is driven by a warming atmosphere holding more moisture, resulting in extreme, high-intensity thunderstorms,” because too much fossil fuel CO2 brings too much atmospheric heat.

“Tipping pointsin climate science are well-defined: they occur when a series of interlinked changes amplify one another until the whole system becomes unstable and shifts uncontrollably into a different state. Loss ofsea iceat the poles, for example, reduces the amount of sunlight reflected into space, further heating Earth’s surface, which then accelerates ice loss—a vicious cycle that defines the tipping process.” (Anders Kevernann)

Climate free fall instability is already evident throughout the world, for example, roughly 33% of global land surface is in drought versus 10-12% in 2000. That’s a tripling of average global drought in only 25 years. This is worldwide climate free fall in earnest. Hence, the economic impact for food production and habitability is threatened well before exceeding tipping points.

The highest risk periods for the world economic systems occur during the big transition period to tipping points: “The period between passing the tipping temperature and reaching the full tipping point—when system collapse becomes inevitable—is whenweather fluctuationsincrease most dramatically. This transition could unfold over decades, creating a prolonged period ofeconomic disruption.” (Anders Kevernann)

Tipping points are triggered by “temperatures” beyond pre-industrial. For example, ‘temperature trigger points’ for key ecosystems, Source:Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research: (1) Greenland Ice Sheet triggers at 1.5C (2) Arctic Winter Sea Ice 4.5C (3) Boreal Permafrost 4C (4) West Antarctic Ice Sheet 1.5C (5) East Antarctic Ice Sheet 7.5C (6) East Antarctica Subglacial Basins 3C, (7) AMOC 4C (8) Labrador-Irminger Sea Convection 1.8C (9) Amazon Rainforest 3.5C.

As outlined, some of the most vulnerable, most impactful climate systems are already knocking on the door. The consequences for sea level rise could be devastating, catching society unaware, unprepared.

Because the anarchists were totally atheistic, and I donвҖҷt like that. ThatвҖҷs why we call ourselves anarcho-chвҖҷixiвҲ’stained anarchists. вҖӘOur anarchism is not pure. This essay utilizes an anarcho-indigenous/mestiz@ lens to explore how the GuaranГӯ concept of tekoвҖҷa (tekoha) (settlement/village/community) can lead to different formations of the ways in which we relate to each other and to the earth. вҖӘIt is both a philosophical inquiry that aims to challenge the nation-state and capitalism, and also a practice of speculative geographies that imagines possible futures along with the creation вҖӘвҖӘof вҖңa new world in the shell of the oldвҖқ вҖӘвҖӘinspired by Indigenous epistemologies. вҖӘMy aim is to demonstrate that the GuaranГӯ language is a bearer of revolutionary philosophical concepts and, as such, is a form of counterpower present in the lives of all GuaranГӯ speakers, Indigenous and mestiz вҖӘ@вҖӘ alike.вҖӘ My hope is to suggest ways to radicalize Paraguayan mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@вҖӘ вҖӘ identity through the manifested revolutionary counterpower of the GuaranГӯ language, in the effort to replace the Paraguayan state, a political formation whose existence is maintained by the colonialist persistence of a depoliticized mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@вҖӘ вҖӘ identity, with another form of polity.вҖӘ

вҖӘThis essay is a philosophical exercise rooted in вҖӘ вҖӘava ГұeвҖҷeвҖӘ вҖӘ, the GuaranГӯ language. The point is not to make assertions about First Nation societies pre-European contact, nor to try to glean some understanding about social order from historical texts written at the time of conquest. The aim is to look specifically for GuaranГӯ epistemologies that reside within the language itself and to assess how these words point to concepts that describe a way of being in the world. Thus, my aim is not to fix, and therefore make these concepts вҖңknowable,вҖқ but rather to try to draw a way of thinking and being from the philosophy of my ancestors and Paraguayan culture. вҖӘ

вҖӘI am acutely aware that to begin from an anti-colonial position and, at the same time, to demand that Indigenous epistemologies be recognized as philosophically rich within the academy is already an irreconcilable contradiction. In truth, however, what I want is for the academy to cease to exist, and to see in its place a multiplicity of fluid spaces, ways of knowing, and ways of being that create material change in the world that are not solely rooted in European ways of thinking or being.вҖӘ

вҖӘFor this essay, I have employed a compound methodology that engages both autoethnographic practices along with speaking with Paraguayans predominantly in Asuncion. My autoethnographic practice is grounded in my own dislocation from my culture, having emigrated to the вҖӘвҖӘusвҖӘвҖӘ at the age of eight. My autoethnographic process, in relation to the GuaranГӯ language, is informed by my experience of reading GuaranГӯ dictionaries and discovering on my own that the words have deep philosophical meanings, and then taking those ideas back to my family members and other Paraguayans and having them corroborate my findings. It is clear that the ways in which mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@вҖӘ вҖӘ identities are contemporarily formed in the вҖӘвҖӘusвҖӘвҖӘ and in South America (in this case specifically Paraguay) are quite different, and this difference has also informed my way of thinking. Immigrating to the вҖӘвҖӘusвҖӘвҖӘ as a child propelled me into a process of being racialized in a way that I would not have experienced had I remained in Paraguay. Through this process of racialization and othering that Latin@ immigrants experience in the вҖӘвҖӘusвҖӘвҖӘ, and its relation to the violence of structural racism, I came to understand that not only did I, my family members, and my countryfolk have an ancestral pre-colonial language that we spoke and whose concepts were part of our culture, but that we were actively being perceived as people of Indigenous descent. This created a rupture in my own identity in relation to that of my family and countryfolk who stayed back home and were not racialized by the same processes and thus tend to actively self-identify as either a depoliticized concept of mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@вҖӘ вҖӘ or, rather strangely, as white. In my practice, both as an artist and writer, I feel pressured to coherently define myself in relationship to the legacy of colonization; this is an impulse I actively reject, however, and much of my thinking is informed by this refusal. This process of self and outsider identification fascinates me, both in its specificity as it relates to Paraguay, but also in the broader ways that it defines these marked differences of Latin@ identity between the north and the south. Perhaps the ways in which mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@sвҖӘ вҖӘ identify themselves in the north marks a total conceptual break from the ways in which mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@sвҖӘ вҖӘ were identified and came to self-identify in the south, and this incoherence may be frustratingвҖӘ; [2] for me, however, it is laden with a positive tension. It вҖӘвҖӘraises the question of the desire for coherence, on whose terms and for what purpose? And, ultimately, the question of what political logic coherence in these identities would serve?вҖӘ

вҖӘFor the purposes of this text, I would like to blur the definitions or understandings of the distinct meanings between the concepts of mestizo and GuaranГӯ (Indigenous) identities in Paraguay. There is both a biological and a cultural dimension to mestiz@ identity (Cadena (de la), 2006), and I find both to be important. What I am most interested in for this text, however, is the part of Paraguayan mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@вҖӘ вҖӘ identity that is defined by its relationship to GuaranГӯ culture through the overwhelming use of the GuaranГӯ language by Paraguayan people, along with the implications of mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@вҖӘ вҖӘ Paraguayans claiming GuaranГӯ roots without the ethical ramifications that I feel this identity necessitates. What I came to realize through my interviews is that ethnic self-identification in Paraguay has more to do with class than with biological conceptions of race. Upper-class, educated people tend to primarily identify as whites of European descent despite phenotypical characteristics that may point to a different version of this narrative. When questioned about their identity further, they either begrudgingly identify as mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@s вҖӘ вҖӘwith Indigenous ancestry, or more often simply identify as Paraguayans. This contrasts with the working-class and working poor people I interviewed (domestic workers, street vendors, field hands) who without exception or hesitation identify as people of Indigenous, specifically GuaranГӯ, descent.вҖӘ

The interviews that I conducted were done for the purposes of collecting material for a documentary film about the medicinal plant culture of Paraguay, вҖӘ pohГў Гұana . [3] вҖӘFor this film project, I interviewed Paraguayans from all walks of life, including: вҖӘ вҖӘyuyer@sвҖӘ вҖӘ (sellers of plant medicine), a broad range of Paraguayans from different class backgrounds, GuaranГӯ activists, and Western medicine doctors. The bulk of the interviews were conducted in Asuncion, though a number of interviews were collected in the south-eastern region of Paraguay, in the rural areas surrounding the town of San Cosme y San Damian. Part of what I am exploring with this documentary are the ways in which this practice of plant medicine, rooted in ancestral Indigenous knowledge, characterizes mestizвҖӘ вҖӘ@вҖӘ вҖӘ identity in Paraguay, along with the tensions this practice of вҖӘ вҖӘpohвҖӘГў вҖӘГұanaвҖӘ вҖӘ manifests in Paraguayan identities. It was through this dual process of investigating the language and the medicinal plant use that I reached many of my conclusions. My background is in the arts, predominantly film and photography, so I am very aware that the methods I use for my work are decidedly outside of the boundaries of what is accepted as the вҖңclassical methodologyвҖқ of the discipline of anthropology. вҖӘГ‘вҖӘвҖӘamandвҖӘвҖӘГәвҖӘ вҖӘ, the First One, created words in the darkness before the earth: вҖӘ вҖӘava вҖӘвҖӘГұвҖӘвҖӘeвҖҷвҖӘвҖӘбәҪвҖӘ вҖӘ, the language of the people (Cadogan, 1959: 13вҖ‘16). Words, for the GuaranГӯ, are a sacred creationвҖ”speech, a sacred act. вҖӘ вҖӘГ‘eвҖҷeвҖӘ вҖӘ, words/speech, is the manifestation of вҖӘ вҖӘГұeвҖҷaвҖӘ вҖӘ, the soul. Speech is the means through which вҖӘ вҖӘГұeвҖҷaвҖӘ вҖӘ is expressed and comes to be known.вҖӘ

вҖӘAva ГұeвҖҷeвҖӘ вҖӘ, the Indigenous language of the GuaranГӯ people, is spoken by over eight million people throughout Paraguay, southern Brazil, southern Bolivia, northern Argentina, and in the GuaranГӯ/mestiz@ diaspora throughout the world. GuaranГӯ philosophy is rooted in its language. In вҖӘ вҖӘava ГұeвҖҷe,вҖӘ вҖӘ the meanings of words shift and expand as they are fused together with other words. For example, вҖӘ вҖӘtekoвҖӘ вҖӘ means вҖңto be, a state of being, our way of being, our culture, oneвҖҷs temperament,вҖқ вҖӘ вҖӘsвҖӘвҖӘГЈвҖӘ вҖӘ means вҖңrope, chain, obstacle, slavery,вҖқ and вҖӘ вҖӘsвҖӘвҖӘГөвҖӘ вҖӘ means вҖңto cut or break.вҖқ вҖӘ вҖӘTeko sвҖӘвҖӘГЈвҖӘвҖӘsвҖӘвҖӘГөвҖӘ вҖӘ is how you say вҖңfree / the state of being free,вҖқ but it literally means вҖңto exist in a state of cutting that which binds you or keeps you from being free,вҖқ which implies that to be free requires actionвҖӘ. [5] The concept is a lesson both for the one who seeks their freedomвҖ”you must take action and вҖңcutвҖқ that which keeps you from being freeвҖ”and for the one who seeks to take someoneвҖҷs freedomвҖ”your actions have put you on the wrong side of the GuaranГӯ blade and you will be cut. вҖӘIn GuaranГӯ epistemology, to exist freely requires you to actively resist oppression. Within this framework, you yourself are responsible for your emancipation, and the construction of a state of freedom is a constant act of engaging with forces that keep you from being free.

Another example is the word вҖӘ вҖӘyvyraвҖӘ вҖӘ. вҖӘ вҖӘYvyвҖӘ вҖӘ means вҖңsoil / earth / the world,вҖқ and вҖӘ вҖӘraвҖӘ вҖӘ means вҖңit will becomeвҖқ or вҖңit will be.вҖқ вҖӘ вҖӘYvyraвҖӘ вҖӘ is how you say вҖңtreeвҖқ in GuaranГӯ, but the deeper meaning is вҖңthat which will become the earth.вҖқ вҖӘThis conceptualization of a tree sees it not as singular identifiable entity that is limited by its structure, but rather as something whose existence radiates outward to encompass concepts beyond its form and which is conceived as a process of transformation. Both the tree and the earth are in a perpetual process of becoming; they exist while simultaneously coming into being. They are alive, they have their own integrity and their own trajectoryвҖ”the concept of the future exists within their physical frameвҖ”they are here now and they will be here later.

вҖӘUnderstanding that trees have a future, and that this future is to become the earth, has important temporal significances. Both the tree and the earth that it becomes are a way of seeing time laid bare before you. They co-exist in the same moment, and they come from and to each other, and in this way, time is understood as a cycle of which we too are a part. By destroying a tree, we prevent the future earth from coming into existence. How much of this preventing of the future earth can we bear? If the forest is the place from which the earth is made, then what does its destruction mean? Does the future earth have a right to exist?вҖӘ

The history of colonization in Paraguay is too long to properly cover in this short essay, but I would like to point to a specific moment where the GuaranГӯ language was attacked: in 1870, it was prohibited to use the GuaranГӯ language in Paraguayan schools. вҖӘIn his essay вҖңThe Bicentenary of Paraguayan Independence and the GuaranГӯ Language,вҖқ вҖӘвҖӘMiguel вҖӘвҖӘГҒвҖӘвҖӘngel VerГіn GГіmez вҖӘвҖӘnotes that вҖңcorporal and psychological punishments inflicted on the children for speaking in school the only language they knew included, among other things, slaps on the mouth, detention during recess, canings, insults, and name-callingвҖқ (вҖӘвҖӘGГіmezвҖӘвҖӘ, 2013: 407вҖ“408).вҖӘвҖӘ VerГіn GГіmezвҖӘвҖӘ further elucidates that the punishments doled out for speaking GuaranГӯ had caused a вҖңgenuine social mutism, with serious effects on the collective self-confidence of the Paraguayan peopleвҖқ (вҖӘ вҖӘibid.вҖӘ вҖӘ).вҖӘ

вҖӘIn вҖӘ вҖӘava ГұeвҖҷe,вҖӘ вҖӘ words and combination of words function as profound concepts that teach one how to be in the world. The language is passed down from one generation to the nextвҖӘ. вҖӘIt is free, and it belongs to everyone. And yet, whenever I am in Paraguay, I hear people deride the GuaranГӯ language. In 2015, while in AsunciГіn, I spoke to an upper-class, university educated man who works with livestock for a living. He expressed frustration in his field because, despite the fact that he tried to bring scientific advances into animal husbandry in Paraguay, he was never able to go as far as he wanted to because he could not properly train people. He explained, вҖңThe problem with Paraguay is the GuaranГӯ language. We will never advance until we stop speaking GuaranГӯ because GuaranГӯ is a limited language.вҖқ For this man, GuaranГӯ is an obstacle вҖӘвҖӘthat the Paraguayan people must overcomeвҖӘ. TвҖӘhis is not a rare sentiment amongst upper-class mestiz@s and whites. Yet Paraguayan identity is defined by its GuaranГӯ rootsвҖ”so much so that calling someone вҖңвҖӘ вҖӘParaguayoвҖӘ вҖӘвҖқ or вҖңвҖӘ вҖӘParaguayaвҖӘ вҖӘвҖқ can function as a racial slur in the countries that border Paraguay. Similarly to how Colombian identity is linked to the drug trade in the international imaginationвҖӘ, вҖӘParaguayan identity is stigmatized by its GuaranГӯ characteristics, and is seen as inherently backwards or primitive. These learned values are the continued expression of colonial violence in the ways that we conceive of ourselves and others of Indigenous descent as somehow less than the European invaders.вҖӘ

вҖӘMestiz@ identity, in the end, has little to do with biology or blood and more to do with a proximity to certain concepts. Mestiz@ identity, in the Paraguayan context, relates to the subjectвҖҷs capacity to fit into the logic of colonization, the state, and capitalismвҖ”and thus ensure their ability to act in service of, and be governed by that logic (Cadena de la, вҖӘ вҖӘop. cit.вҖӘ вҖӘ: 60). Mestiz@, then, is a political formulation born out of the centralized powerвҖҷs need to categorize people in order to be able to rule over them. The concept of the mestiz@ as an identity that is separate from oneвҖҷs Indigenous roots is one of the triumphs of colonization in Latin America. The success of the вҖңвҖӘ вҖӘmestizajeвҖӘ вҖӘвҖқ as a somehow singular identity that has deeper connections to its European roots is the crown jewel of colonial logic, as it does the work of separating вҖңmixed raceвҖқ people from their relations, their territory, and the responsibilities that link people to the land and to each other. And, it is these learned values that manifest themselves as the denial of the material expression of Indigenous philosophies in our shared world.вҖӘ

вҖӘIn a society, which is hierarchical, held up by armies, police, punitive deterrents and authoritarian-based respectability, the human as variable does not need to be considered.

вҖӘMy great-grandmotherвҖҷs house was built directly on top of the hard red earthвҖӘ. [7] There were walls, a roof, doors, windows, a stove, a large clay jug filled with well water, places to eat, sit, and sleep, along with all the other things that make a houseвҖ”except for a floor; there was no floor. I remember watching her sweep the top layer of red earth out the front door every evening, making the surface smooth again, erasing the scars of the day. вҖӘ

вҖӘChe pyвҖҷaho вҖӘ вҖӘis how you say вҖңI rememberвҖқ in вҖӘ вҖӘava ГұeвҖҷeвҖӘ . [8] A literal translation would be, вҖңit goes to my heart / soul.вҖқвҖӘвҖӘ [9] In GuaranГӯ, then, to remember is to engage with the things that have gone to your soul. It is not that simple, of course, but it is a good place to begin. For Indigenous and mestiz@ peoples, to remember is an act of resistance to colonization. To resist is to act in opposition to that which oppresses you. There is movement in memoryвҖ”it creates as it resists. To remember is not simply an individual, internal process, but a way for a people to return to a place of being, of having been, and to become again. By engaging with memory and trying to regain what they have lost through the violence of colonization, Indigenous and mestiz@ peoples build their world anew by relying on their own concepts and ways of being in the world, while actively rejecting the violently imposed narratives of colonizationвҖӘ. [10]

вҖӘWithin the power dynamics of colonizing empires and settler-colonial states, the memories of the colonized, the enslaved, and those who were somehow othered are held captive by the official, sanctioned memories of the empire and settler-colonial powers. By its very nature, the nation-state is a centralizing force and thus aims to control and shape the narrative of its own genesis and continuation, which requires the snuffing out of counternarratives. The need for a total, linear, hierarchical narrative is indicative of the broader machinations of nation-states: coercive control obtained and maintained through brutal violence or the constant threat of violence.вҖӘ

вҖӘAlso necessary for state power are subjectsвҖ”more commonly euphemized as вҖңcitizensвҖқвҖ”for subjugation. In order for citizens to be useful to the State apparatus, they must be as homogenous as possible, as homogeneity is a prerequisite for control. To accumulate power, one must to do away with difference вҖӘвҲ’ вҖӘdifferent people, different ways of thinking, different ways of being вҖӘвҲ’ вҖӘbecause they challenge powerвҖҷs fundamental need to be monolithic and centralized. While there are always characteristics that make citizens different from each other, at their core, they must вҖӘ вҖӘbelieveвҖӘ вҖӘ themselves to be a single entity in order to function as a вҖңmajority,вҖқ and thus be governable. It is for this reason that Indigenous Peoples, by their very existence, are in opposition to the nation-stateвҖӘ; вҖӘthey will never be citizens of and for a settler-colonial power. The naming of them as Indigenous Peoples alone puts them in opposition to state power: first, by recalling a moment before the existence of the nation-state, and second, by aligning them with an exceptional tribal sovereignty that is inevitably multiple and decentralized and thus inherently resistant to the fixity required in order to be governed. Mestiz@ identity, I would argue, is a collapsing of identities and concepts in the hopes of creating the conditions for a people to identify as citizens of a nation state and consent to be being governed.вҖӘ

вҖӘAnarchist anthropologists Pierre Clastres, David Graeber, Andrej вҖӘGrubaДҚiДҮвҖӘ, Silvia Rivera Cusicanqui, James C. Scott, and RaГәl Zibechi, amongst others, have written extensively about societies of people who, throughout the world and human history, have imagined and manifested power in ways that actively reject hierarchy and centralization. Through their writings, these anthropologists have challenged the notion that centralized power is necessary for societies to function, along with the idea that the manifestation of the state is somehow a part of an evolutionary trajectory of human organization, implying that the societies that actively reject a state formation are somehow primitive compared to societies that have states. Colonization and the subsequent formation of settler-colonial states has punished (and continues to punish) the GuaranГӯ and other Indigenous Peoples through a process of violent dispossession of their cultures, languages, epistemologies, land, and lives by cloaking unmatched brutality in a false logic of cultural dominance and systemic criminalizationвҖ”often couched as Christian benevolenceвҖ”which has created the nation-state dominated world that we live in now.вҖӘ

вҖӘIt feels important to state that anarchism is not a Eurocentric idea, but rather is the name given to a tendency that has manifested itself throughout time and throughout the world and has been called by many other names. What these anarchist anthropologists examine are social organizations that escape/refuse the logic of colonization, the state, and capitalism, and that, through that refusal, create possibilities for people to reimagine and reinvent the ways that they relate to each other, other living things, the land, and their collective futures.вҖӘ

вҖӘFor GuaranГӯ speakers, the GuaranГӯ language holds an active possibility for this sort of collective reinvention, and this tension is ever-present as the language is spoken. The purpose of this anarchoвҖ‘anticolonial project, that seeks to problematize mestiz@ Paraguayan identity in relation to the GuaranГӯ language, is not for a total or comprehensive recovery of ancestral GuaranГӯ traditions, understandings, or way of life, but rather a process of possible reinvention that would stand in opposition to the oppressive manifestations of the Paraguayan state, along with challenging the incongruences of mestiz@ Paraguayan identity that I perceive to function in service of the colonial process. вҖӘ

вҖӘThe memory of GuaranГӯ epistemologies lives hidden in its language. вҖӘ вҖӘAva ГұeвҖҷeвҖӘ вҖӘ is the manifested counterpower of the Paraguayan/GuaranГӯ people, not only because it continues to be spoken, despite of hundreds years of colonization, but also because it retains within it ancestral philosophical concepts that teach of ways to resist domination while revealing alternative ways of being in the world. To remember is to resist subjugation, erasure, and genocide through a process of collective enunciations and becomings. For one to be able to become through memory, one must return to the minor, fluid, and shifting. To borrow an вҖӘвҖӘimage from Deleuze and Guattari, memory is like grass: [11] to kill it, you have to kill all the animals that ate its seeds or carry it in their coats, pulverize every node of its rhizomatic roots, sterilize the soil and make it inert, because everything remembers. The organ of memory lives outside the body in the mind of the collective. Memory is a communal energy that is powerful because it is ever-shifting, contradictory, incoherent, and free.вҖӘ

After creating what would become human language amidst the primordial fog, in the darkness before the earth, the next thing the First One created was mborayhu, the foundation of love, which was love for one another, or solidarity.вҖӘ In retellings and translations of the story of creation, вҖӘ вҖӘmborayhuвҖӘ вҖӘ is translated as вҖңвҖӘ вҖӘamor al prвҖӘвҖӘГівҖӘвҖӘjimoвҖӘ вҖӘвҖқвҖӘвҖӘ (вҖӘвҖӘвҖңlove thy neighborвҖқвҖӘвҖӘ), вҖӘвҖӘwhich points to a Christian conceptвҖӘ. [12] The early texts from the era of first contact between the GuaranГӯ and the European invaders inevitably have a religious overtone because conversion to Christianity was an integral part of colonization.вҖӘ вҖӘYet, as Pierre Clastres points out inвҖӘ вҖӘ La Palabra Luminosa, вҖӘ вҖӘвҖңthe missionaries adopted the word mborayhu to express the Christian idea of вҖҳlove thy neighborвҖҷ, just as they took the name of Tupan, the elemental of tempest and thunder, to stand in for the name of GodвҖқ (Clastres, 1993: 35). Clastres further elucidates that, вҖңif, consequently, вҖӘ вҖӘmborayhuвҖӘ вҖӘ was able to mean love one another, it is because the word already expressed the idea of tribal solidarityвҖқ (вҖӘ вҖӘibid.вҖӘ вҖӘ: 35).вҖӘ

In The Land-Without-Evil , вҖӘHвҖӘГ©вҖӘlвҖӘГЁвҖӘne вҖӘClastres makes the point that the imposition of capitalism upon the GuaranГӯ made it impossible for them to continue practicing their traditional spirituality because the dynamic forced upon them by capitalism is diametrically opposed to the principles of mborayhu . The remunerative nature of capitalismвҖ”in which one sells oneвҖҷs labor (literally, the moments oneвҖҷs life is made of) for the ability to purchase material goods necessary for survival, such as food, housing, and medicineвҖ”created an ontological rift between the old ways and the settler-colonial way of life, which is structured around the maintaining of the nation-state by funneling resources to a central power. вҖӘShe writes, вҖңThis new system demands that each individual work for himself. вҖӘIn other terms, the whole ethic of mborayhu is being questioned. вҖӘNot only has the leisure to sing been lost with economic autarchy, but also the entire system of exchange that exactly defined society.вҖқвҖӘвҖӘ(Clastres, 1995: 97). вҖӘIn a videotaped interview for Casa de AmГ©rica, Bartomeu MeliГ , a philosopher and longtime student of GuaranГӯ epistemologies, вҖӘidentifies the three primary defining qualities of GuaranГӯ culture as its language, its spirituality (which is based on the GuaranГӯ language), and its economic system (which conceives of selling things as a form of vengeance)вҖӘ. [13] вҖӘMeliГ  further illuminates the economic system of the GuaranГӯ by clarifying:

They still maintain it, though it is somewhat hiddenвҖ”their reciprocal economic system in which there is no proper buying and selling of things. To buy and sell things in their language means to seek vengeance. When you sell something you are taking vengeance on someone, instead of giving it to them you are being vengeful. Of course, once vengeance begins the other has the right to be vengeful against you. For them, we are a society of vengefulness... all the world is being vengeful to someone. We are a society of repeating successive vengeances. [14]

The taking away of a PeopleвҖҷs autonomy to relate to the each other and to the earth as dictated by their cultural practices is a defining component of colonization and an absolute necessity for the perpetuation of state power and capitalism. In this case, the imposition of an economic system based on monetary exchange in the service of a centralized power served as a way to delegitimize GuaranГӯ concepts of exchange that were deeply rooted in the philosophies that, for them, came into being at the time of creation and define their very existence on this earth. It is deeply important that the first two creations in the GuaranГӯ cosmogony are language and solidarity. Both of these are communal practices, and their primacy serves to reveal the centrality of community for the GuaranГӯ. Language and solidarity are integral to the survival of a People as an independent culture that stands in resistance to genocide and assimilation.

Sovereignty requires more than language and loveвҖ”it also necessitates land. Land or territoryвҖ”a place to be in community and practice oneвҖҷs cultureвҖ”is of critical importance to Indigenous Peoples, and the removal from oneвҖҷs place of being has been a weapon of genocide that settler-colonial governments have used against Indigenous Peoples since first contact. вҖӘThe GuaranГӯ notion of spatiality and territoriality is expressed in the concept of вҖӘ вҖӘtekoвҖҷaвҖӘ вҖӘ. вҖӘ вҖӘTekoвҖӘ вҖӘ means вҖңway of being,вҖқ вҖңstate of existence,вҖқ or вҖңculture,вҖқ and вҖӘ вҖӘhaвҖӘ вҖӘ or вҖҳвҖӘ вҖӘaвҖӘ вҖӘ means вҖңhere/this place.вҖқ вҖӘ вҖӘTekoвҖҷaвҖӘ вҖӘ is colloquially translated as вҖңvillage,вҖқ but the deeper meaning is вҖңthe place where we can be who we are as a people,вҖқ or вҖңthe place where we (the GuaranГӯ) can practice our cultural ways.вҖқ вҖӘThus, embedded within the concept of tekoвҖҷa is the idea that to be a person or community requires a sovereign place where one can practice what it means to be GuaranГӯ. вҖӘWithout land there is no вҖӘ вҖӘteko вҖӘ вҖӘ(MeliГ , 2016: 45). вҖӘFor the GuaranГӯ, the act of being and becoming requires a relationship to the land; without this bond, there is no way to be GuaranГӯ. вҖӘAs Bartomeu MeliГ  points out, вҖңspatiality is fundamental to GuaranГӯ culture [because] it assures their freedom and their ability to maintain their ethnic identity.вҖқвҖӘ [15] вҖӘSimply put:вҖӘ to be GuaranГӯ, you need other GuaranГӯ people, your language, freedom to practice your culture, and a place to exist that supports and nourishes your way of life. Within вҖӘ вҖӘtekoвҖҷa,вҖӘ вҖӘ the concept of selfhood is intimately dependent on the presence of your relations and a relationship to the landвҖ”one cannot be whole and be alone without community or land.вҖӘ

TekoвҖҷa is place defined by ways of beingвҖ”by relationships, and not by territorial borders. This is a radically different conception of place than is held in Western society. This concept of place is alive, and not easily mappable or fixed. The GuaranГӯ concept of place is bound by relationships between people and their spiritual and cultural practices in relation to other living things on an animate Earth.

вҖӘThe belief in an active constructing of a world in which вҖӘ вҖӘtekoвҖҷa вҖӘ вҖӘcan exist is, at its core, about resistance: resistance to alienation, colonial suffering, loss of culture, and death вҖӘвҲ’ вҖӘand resistance is what makes the world anew. Resistance has a spatial dimension (a territory in which one can be free) and a temporal dimension (the future, in which one is free). To resist is to believe in the existence of a future вҖӘвҲ’ вҖӘto resist is to become.вҖӘ

вҖӘAnders Burman, in вҖӘ вҖӘIndigeneity and Decolonization in the Bolivian AndesвҖӘ вҖӘ, writes, вҖңOntological dimensions of human existence are at the center of struggles over resources and power since these are simultaneously struggles over meaning, struggles over reality, over вҖҳwhat there is.вҖҷвҖқ(Burman, 2016: 12) The conflicting ontologies of вҖңwhat there isвҖқ is really what it comes down to. If when we look at a place and see that вҖңwhat there isвҖқ as a forest filled with raw materials to process and sell as fuel, as opposed to a multitude of individual beings in the process of becoming the earth, then the harsh dichotomy of these opposing ways of seeing and being in the world becomes overwhelmingly evident. вҖӘBeing from a community whose philosophies are marginalized through the processes of colonization means that your communitiesвҖ”your peopleвҖ”are marginal too, and the marginalization of a people, the assigning of people and the places where those people live as not valuable, comes with devastating consequences.

вҖӘThe contemporary GuaranГӯ practice of вҖӘ вҖӘtekoвҖҷaвҖӘ вҖӘ is engaged with GuaranГӯ futurity, as opposed to any conceptualizations of the practice that would align it with a desire to replicate a pre-colonial GuaranГӯ existence. The act of bringing into being вҖӘ вҖӘtekoвҖҷaвҖӘ вҖӘ by a people still experiencing colonization is a practice of prefigurative politicsвҖӘвҖ”вҖӘof living in the world in the manner in which one actually chooses and is in line with oneвҖҷs ethics, as opposed to living in the world as it is prescribed by coercive state and capitalist-based power structures, that, in the case of Indigenous Peoples, outwardly deny the possibility of their existence by seeking their extermination. The concept of вҖӘ вҖӘtekoвҖҷaвҖӘ вҖӘ centers important questions about identity, the right to practice oneвҖҷs culture, and the often violent clashes over rights to the land. вҖӘThe subject of land in Paraguay is one marked by turmoil and bloodshed. The sixteenth-century colonial policies of Indigenous dispossession continue, under a different guise, to this day. Indigenous Peoples and mestiz@ campesin@ communities in rural Paraguay have been forcibly displaced by large landowners who use their lands for big profit monoculture cultivation projects, cattle ranching, and housing developments. And the Paraguayan wilderness is being cleared at an alarming rate for these projects. вҖӘYet, the apparent incongruity between Indigenous and mestiz@ identities has the potential to become a positive tensionвҖ”one that creates (rather than negates) GuaranГӯ futurity. вҖӘ вҖӘTekoвҖҷaвҖӘ вҖӘ is resistance to the present in service of the future, which, if embraced by Indigenous and mestiz@ people alike in the GuaranГӯ world, and allowed to exist and to flourish, would radically transform the landscape of peopleвҖҷs everyday lives, as anarchists like to say, in ways that reflect the world they would actually like to live in.вҖӘ

вҖӘWe lack creation. We lack resistance to the present. The creation of concepts in itself calls for a future form, for a new earth and a people that do not yet exist. Europeanization does not constitute a becoming but merely the history of capitalism, which prevents the becoming of subjected peoples.

вҖӘThe Indigenous Peoples and Nations of the Americas are the memory of our future, if they did not exist, we would have to invent them, as we find that we are also in the process of inventing ourselves anew.

вҖӘAva ГұeвҖҷбәҪвҖӘ вҖӘ is a rebellious language. It moves. It mutates. It refuses to die. ItвҖҷs always speaking and it creates new territory as it is spoken. Struggling to learn it from a distance, I have come to know that every word, every phrase, is a world of its own, and these worlds collide to form concepts that feel urgently necessary in our time of world-wide crisis wrought by unmitigated industrialization and subsequent man-made climate catastrophes. GuaranГӯ philosophy is immanent in вҖӘ вҖӘava ГұeвҖҷбәҪ вҖӘ вҲ’ вҖӘit lives in the words, just as it was meant to when the language came into being in the darkness before the earth. I want to draw these ancestral ideas into the present and make the case that these philosophies recenter themselves in the construction of the southвҖ”both in the restructuring of identities and also in recreating different ways of relating to the land, to each other, and to the future. вҖӘвҖӘI want to live through a change in the concept of love and have it mean solidarity, so we can know what it is like to live in a place where вҖңto beвҖқ means taking care of each other and the earth, in which we collectively seek to transform our conditions and create a way of being that strives towards вҖӘ вҖӘtekojoja вҖӘ вҖӘвҲ’ вҖңвҖӘвҖӘjustice,вҖӘвҖӘвҖқвҖӘвҖӘ or вҖңthe state in which things are equal / reciprocal.вҖқ It is time to make way for new ontologies вҖӘвҲ’ вҖӘfor futures and becomings that have always been there and that challenge the Euro-colonial capitalist model that we are captives of today.вҖӘ

вҖӘConcepts matter because our philosophies shape the world. It is our philosophies that make the lives of some people unlivableвҖӘ, вҖӘthat make the lives of our non-human relations unlivableвҖӘ, вҖӘthat render our future an unlivable place. How we collectively conceive of somethingвҖ”even something as seemingly simple as a treeвҖ”is of incredible consequence. We must make space for other philosophies and concepts that can bring into being new intensities that challenge oppressive power and its manifestation in our social constructions and our material practices. How different might our architecture and urban design, our solutions to questions of resource extraction and distribution, or our approach to everyday life be if we rooted our actions in philosophies that valued our relationship to each other and to the earth? I, for one, am curious to see the GuaranГӯ response to the questions of urbanism, the DinГ© solutions to the problematics of access to water, the Mbororo Fulani vision on the issue of property, the Kurdish answer to the complexities of self-government, the Maori understandings of the concept of family, and endless other multiplicities and possibilities of thought and materialityвҖӘ. [18]

вҖӘThis calls to mind Maia RamnathвҖҷs critically under-read book, вҖӘ вҖӘDecolonizing Anarchism: An Antiauthoritarian History of IndiaвҖҷs Liberation StruggleвҖӘ вҖӘ, wherein she writes:вҖӘ

вҖӘWith a small вҖӘ вҖӘaвҖӘ вҖӘ, the word anarchism implies a set of assumptions and principles, a recurrent tendency or orientation вҖӘвҲ’ вҖӘwith the stress on movement in a direction, not a perfected conditionвҖ”toward more dispersed and less concentrated power; less top-down hierarchy and more self-determination through bottom-up participation; liberty and equality seen as directly rather than inversely proportional; the nurturance of individuality and diversity within a matrix of interconnectivity, mutuality, and accountability; and an expansive recognition of the various forms that power relations can take, and correspondingly, the various dimensions of emancipation.вҖӘ [19]

вҖӘSelf-organizing communities all over the world, Indigenous or not, and explicitly anarchist or not, are already engaging with solutions to the problems we face and the crises that loom on the horizonвҖӘ; tвҖӘhe question is whether these solutions, these intensities, will be allowed to fully come into being.вҖӘ

вҖӘI am in no way calling for a вҖңbecoming indigenous.вҖқ This is not possible. Colonization has already strained the relationships that Indigenous Peoples have with each other, not to mention the incongruity among Indigenous Peoples and settlers. The issue is not for an individual to become something they are not, but rather for a centering of other ways of being in the world that may make our final years on this planet вҖӘвҲ’ вҖӘhowever many or few there may be вҖӘвҲ’ вҖӘones worth living. We cannot transcend this earth. We cannot get out of it and expect to remain who we are. But we can open new territories for Indigenous epistemologies to make real change in the world, and we can resist domination and coercion while вҖңbuilding a new world in the shell of the old.вҖқ And we can, over time, become a new people on an old earth and come to see that the horizon is all that surrounds us and that the only thing that matters is how we walk toward it togetherвҖӘ. [20]

I've not yet purchased his new book yet, though I'm sure I'll grab a copy sometime, but already all the interviews with him and reviews I've read are fairly infuriating.

His new book The Better Angels of Our Nature: The Decline of Violence in History and Its Causes looks mammoth, much bigger than any of his previous books, about 800 pages. Previous stuff of his like Stuff of Thought and Language Instinct are decent readings in psycholinguistics, while his treading into moral philosophy in The Blank Slate is nauseating.

So far the interviews (see list below) with him reek of whiggish history - 'things are getting better' and violence is on the decline. Of course it depends on how you define violence. So far in most interviews he seems to think violence simply means physical violence, and discounts instituitional violence and the implied threat of physical coercion that exists in all states. Nor does he seem to think poverty and cuts to every aspect of social existence are of importance in discussions of violence.

Some bloggers have already called bullshit on some of his specific claims, though I know little about the history either way - but it's interesting to see historians chellnging him.

His constant misuse of 'anarchy' to describe every situation from drug cartels to Somalia grates every single time he uses it. He has previously made claims in The Blank Slate that he was an anarchist, who loved Bakunin, in the late 60s as a student radical until he witnessed some civil unrest during a police strike in Montreal. He must have a been a really shit anarchist and not taken-in any Bakunin if he thinks drug-cartels and Somalian despots have anything to do with anarchism.

I have no doubt the book will be infuriating, but I'll probably still give it a go next year. Anyone else any thoughts?

Some of Pinkers work is fairly straightforward. For instance, he examined violence among hunter gatherers and early neolithic tribes. A great deal of the evidence he relies on is archaeological e.g. skeletal remains, bone fragments, and other pieces of evidence from excavations, along with standard statistical methodology. The conclusions from his work were that early, pre-state human tribes had much higher levels of violence than we do today (in the western world at least). In particular, he looked at homicide rates (for example, between a quarter and one third of all male deaths among hunter gatherers were attributed to homicide). As far as his use of the term anarchy, I suppose he uses it the same way that everyone else (who's not steeped in anarchist philosophy) uses it, and when you're writing a book, you write in the lexicon of your expected audience. But I do agree, he shouldn't exclude modern warfare from his definition of violence (that's absurd). But I've read other papers, which do factor in modern warfare, and the homicide rate among hunter gatherers is still several thousand times higher than it is today. I guess it does create a kink in the wheel for anarchist theory, assuming you base anarchist theory on a particular view of human nature (which IMO is a very bad idea, since there's so much we have yet to learn on this subject). Anyway, at very minimum, his work (and the work of many others) shows that pre-state societies were very prone to violence, and so apparently we're not the super egalitarian species many imagine us to be (sorry Rousseau). For me the importance of this question isn't to bolster the idea of the nation state as the only organism that can reign in the human propensity for violence, it's important because it shows that humans often resort to violence whether or not we live under a nation state. That Pinker draws certain conclusions from his research (in many cases not his research, but the research of others that he included in his writings) is immaterial. I read the data for myself and draw my own conclusions (or avoid drawing conclusions if unwarranted); and I try not to allow my own affinity for radical theory to bias my interpretation of this sort of research.

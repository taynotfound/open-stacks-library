by João Bernardo From The Commune Reflecting on the recent Israeli aggression against the Mavi Marmara and the feckless impunity with which this country spreads terror in its region, I thought that most commentaries limited themselves to the obvious but fell short of the most important conclusion. Everyone knows that Jews were the victims of great persecution, the Nazis making anti-semitism one of their main ideological bases. From the first day of his regime Hitler persecuted Jews and during the Second World War attempted to exterminate them. It is also widely known that the State of Israel inflicts suffering on the Palestinians, dispossessing them and subjecting them to a system of terror beyond even what the South African racists could achieve in the Apartheid era. Between these two moments: Jews as victims and Israel as aggressor, there is not a contradiction but rather a logical nexus, which this article seeks to explain.

Nasser and Sadat were involved in an anti-British Egyptian military conspiracy with nazi assistance. This is the logic of anti-colonialism/anti-imperialism. Is this supposed to be surprising?

Wasn't trying to deflect well-deserved attention toward the history of zionism, nor was I trying to complain that specific attention was somehow unfair. My point was that virtually every nationalist grouping that had some complaint against the British (and French, and Dutch) Empire during the 1930s and '40s had official and semi-official relations with the Third Reich. Pointing out that mainstream zionists of the left and right were involved with nazi policy makers is not unfair, but removing them from a broader context of anti-colonialism borders on the dishonest. And as Enti pointed out, this focused analysis avoids the principled anti-nazi positions of many zionists. Zionism was not then (although it is much more now) a monolithic entity; for example, prior to WWII there was a vocal minority of secular zionists who refused to attach their zionism to state-building. They are usually called "cultural zionists" and included Buber, Scholem, and Magnes among others. Their project was closely attached to the Hebrew University (unfortunately many of them still shared the anti-Arab racism of their fellow state-oriented zionists).

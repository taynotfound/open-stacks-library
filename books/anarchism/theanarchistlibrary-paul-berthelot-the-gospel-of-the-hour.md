I saw, I listened to a man — who preached in the fields, towns and cities.

And he said: ‘I am not the one who will signal the Hour, but I’ve come to announce the coming Hour.

‘He who will signal the Hour is coming after me — he is greater than I, he is stronger than I.

‘But I know he is going to awake — and it’s then that he will signal the Hour.

‘He will not come to preach harmless words — his sign will be blood and fire.

‘He will cut the throats of the sterile cow — and the bad grain will be cast into the fire.

‘Then many things will be changed, from root to branch — and the first will be mixed up with the last.

‘Fortunate are those who will be ready at that time — for the day of their reign will have come.

‘Fortunate the poor, for they will have nothing to lose and everything to gain. Fortunate the servants, for they will taste the fresh air of freedom.

‘Fortunate those who are hungry now, for they will be filled. Fortunate those who cry today, for they will have reason to laugh.

‘But unfortunate will be those who are not ready — for they will groan, ‘It’s too late! It’s too late!’

‘And some will want to pretend — and try to say: Here I am, I’m ready.

‘But their voice will be shut up in their mouth — and Death will pass onto them.

‘Then unfortunate will be the rich, for they will lose everything. Unfortunate those who command, for no one will obey them.

‘Unfortunate those who stuff themselves to excess, for they will lack even the necessities. Unfortunate above all those who are laughing now, for they will have reason to cry.

‘So I say to you: Prepare yourselves today, for now the Hour is approaching.

‘So that your heart not tremble in your breast — and your mind not be troubled.

‘But that you will rejoice with happiness — and you will know what you have to do.

‘First unbind yourself from personal riches — and don’t dream of working for your profit alone.

‘For he who seeks his personal fortune will lose it — and he who renounces it will find himself rich.

‘For he who wants to be rich will become the enemy of all — and he who says, ‘I have nothing’ will be rich with everything in common.

‘He who wants to work for his profit alone — can do nothing good or lasting.

‘He doesn’t dare plant a tree or build a house — because many others will enjoy it after him, perhaps tomorrow.

‘But he who works for all — he benefits from the work of all.

‘For at that time, nothing will belong to anyone — but all will belong to all.

‘Suppress thoughts of pride and scorn as well — and of domination over your fellowmen.

‘For he who wants to be seated in the first place will be pushed back to the last and mixed with the crowd.

‘And he who wants to be raised above others and command will experience the insult of refusal to obey.

‘Because at that time no one will obey men anymore — but only reason alone.’

And they asked, ‘What is his name, what is his country, and what is this Hour he speaks about?’

And he said, ‘My name is: Someone; my country: the Earth; and the Hour is that of settling the accounts.’

When he was passing through a village, the peasants gathered around him.

And they said to him, ‘You who announce the Hour — tell us what we should then.’

And he told them, ‘When the Hour sounds — come together and rejoice together.

‘Kill the fat pork and the fat cow — and get the good wine from the cellar.

‘And set a large table in the common house — and sit down and enjoy yourselves all together.

‘That whoever lives in his house remain there — and whoever lives in a rented house no longer pay rent.

And whoever has no house summon the others and say to them: Help me build my house.

‘That whoever has a field cultivate it, whoever has an occupation work at it — that the bee give as much as he can of wax and honey.

‘And in the Common House you will have two books wherein each shall come to write:

‘In the first, what he can give — in the second, of what he has need.

‘And give to each what he needs, as far as possible — without considering what he can supply.

‘For the strong don’t get credit for being strong — nor do the weak get blamed for being weak;

‘Nor do the skillful get credit for being skillful — nor are the clumsy at fault for being so.

‘But each ought to be judged according to his own goodwill — who has done what he can is even with all.

‘These things have already been said, but very few of them have been understood — Peace on earth to men of goodwill .

‘And if someone is accused of not doing what he can — or of asking for more than he needs,

‘Let all you mature men and women come together and examine the case with benevolence and charity.

‘And ask him if he wants to give his reasons for acting like that.

‘And if he can’t give any, let him alone — but give him only what is necessary.

‘But if he pretends to have the right to be idle — and to live at the expense of others;

‘Chase him away and let him not come back, as it was said: The idle shall go live elsewhere.’

So the peasants said to him, ‘But our town doesn’t supply everything we need,

‘We need clothes and iron tools — and things that they make only in the city.’

So he asked them, ‘Do you eat all the wheat that you harvest, all the oil that make?’

They answered, ‘No, every year we sell so many sacks of wheat and so many measures of oil.’

So he told them, ‘Therefore you will write to them in the city: Our town can dispose of this wheat and this oil.

‘And you need this and that, and we will make an arrangement.

‘And those from the city will make it possible to give you what you need — seeing that you do what you can to the best of your ability.

‘But in that time many men and women will come to you who will not want to stay in the city.

‘Some with their vain and sterile speech — others wanting to work with you.

‘But you will test them according to the fruits of their labor — observing what their works are.

‘And judging each not by what they say — but by what they do.’

And the peasants talked among themselves — about this Hour that he announced.

While crossing a field he saw a man working with a heavy hoe.

And this man had been laboring for three days — and the field was still not prepared.

So he said to him, ‘Why not work with a plow? Your field would already be ready.’

But the man answered, ‘My field is so small and I am so poor that I can’t work with a plow.’

Now, there were also many other peasants there laboring with hoes;

And he asked them, ‘Why do you work with this heavy old plow and not with the castle’s big one?’

They answered him, ‘Our fields are so small and we are so poor that we can’t rent the castle’s.’

So he told them, ‘When the Hour sounds, break down these walls.’

‘Fill in these ditches, uproot the hedges — and make one single field;

‘And take the big plow from the castle’s shed — and work the big field one time only.

‘And so a few of you will do the work of all — with less trouble.

‘And the others will not lack useful work — for there will be much to do.’

But the peasants asked him, ‘And what will the master of the castle say?’

And he said to them, ‘When the master of the castle hears the sounding of the Hour — his tongue will dry up in his mouth.

‘If he has an evil heart, he will try to flee — but he won’t go far.

‘If he is wise and can accept the inevitable — he will open his doors and lower the bridge of the moat.

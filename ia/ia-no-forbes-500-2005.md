of Power upon the multitude have continually . 
intensified in recent years. As a result of this it more 
often than not seems that we are in a constant state of 
defeat and retreat, becoming less able and less willing 
to confront and transcend the order of Empire. The 
general malaise in socially articulated class struggle . 
within Australia gives proof to the weaknesses of the 
struggle in its capacity to actualise revolt. Or rather, in 
an era characterised by such fierce violence 
administered by the functionaries of Power, how do 
we construct a space whereby the micro-revolts 
undertaken by individuals in their daily lives can 
weave into a social tapestry of revolt sophisticated 
and strong enough to actually reappropriate our own 
lives and communities? For general malaise or no, our 
era is characterised by severe tension, and one force 
within this relationship is constituted by the 
movement of people against the constricting 
discipline of capital, against the permanent state of 
war, and against the racism and destruction of 
contemporary: capitalism. In order to assess how ме

Power, developing over the previous years, into a 
structure of absolute discipline and full spectrum war.

forms of human society, particularly under capitalism 
— from the world wars, the anti-colonial guerrilla 
uprisings and the regional sphere of influence wars of 
cold war imperialism. We now live in the era of 
armed globalisation and the War on Terror. Both of 
these realities position war in a central role within 
everyday social relations, and an inherent, permanent 
and necessary foundation for the sustaining of |

colonisation of everyday life, which has been so 
successful in terms of the occupation of space by 
capital, is the cause of the micro-revolts that occur 
throughout the fabric of Empire. From the smallest 
element of refusal to overt confrontation, a weakness

need then, is to homogenise struggle through the 
construction of formal, leading organisations that 
thrive through the quelling of organic resistance. 
Rather, the struggle needs to define the character of 
any of its organisations. Any such organisation should 
provide a space for the micro-revolts to be shared and 
socialised, for the experience of broader struggles to 
be shared, and to facilitate the development of 
sustainable relationships between the arrays of 
struggles taking place. Organisation is secondary to 
the impetus of struggle. In recognising this, and not 
attempting to rigidify a particular struggle upon the 

. premise, logic and formation of a leading 
organisational body, the common to us all and these 
movements can be identified. In this way resistance:

Biotechnology and Capitalism 


The Corporate Biotech Agenda and the 
Struggle for Democracy 


Global biotech companies argue that agricultural biotechnology is 
all about feeding thé poor, saving the environment and improving 
global health. This high sounding rhetoric disguises the nature of 
capitalism. Corporations' objective is to return a profit to their 
shareholders. Corporations are legally bound to put profit making 
above any other interest.! 


Critics of biotechnology typically adopt a narrow, reformist agenda 
which seeks greater governmental regulation, labeling and more 
stringent safety standards. This cautious approach ignores the 
fundamental danger posed by corporate biotechnology - the 
privatization of every aspect of life. Disguised in debates about 
scientific validity are fundamental questions about who owns and 
controls our lives and our means of subsistence. An increasingly 
small number of global corporations is seeking to dominate the 
entire world food supply. Whether these foods are labeled as 
containing genetically modified organisms or not the danger is the 
exclusion of the majority of human beings from their most 
fundamental rights to food. In their search for new markets the 
biopirates are seeking new acquisitions in the genetic makeup of 
human and non-human beings. 


Biotechnology corporations and their allies in national and 
transnational bodies such as the United States and the World Bank 
are responsible for causing many of the problems which they now 
claim to be trying to solve. The diversion of third world resources 
from subsistence farming into the production of cash crops has been

The green revolution saw the introduction of chemicals into 
agricultural production in many parts of the world, including India. 
Chemical agriculture relies on pumping large amounts of expensive

such as seed saving are proving to be more productive and

monocultures which in turn makes crops more susceptible to insect

attempt to solve the problems of pest and diseases by engineering 
the system to be resistant without the use of external inputs. This 
makes the systems more sustainable and is more effective. | 


Biotechnology as Savior of the Environment 


Biotechnology advocates claim that its adoption will enable us to 
solve a number of pressing environmental issues such as salinity by . 
engineering plants which are tailor made to process these 
environmental pollutants. These claims rest on a single, unstated 
claim about the nature of these problems: that they are technical or 
engineering problems and hence that technology will be adequate to 
fix them. — 


These assumptions are false. They rely upon a reductionist 
interpretation of the natural and social worlds which reduces the 
complexity of human and non-human life to the manipulation of 
genes in a laboratory. These arguments are instrumentalist in that 
they treat non-human life, such as animals and plants, as mere tools 
to be used and abused for the benefit of human beings. Furthermore, 
the advocates of biotechnology, generally in the direct or indirect 
employ of the great biotechnology corporations or the state, mystify 
and obfuscate the Glass conflict that is inherent in these social . 
problems. | 


Attempting to fiddle around the edges of the global ecological crisis 
will not result in significant gains. The problem of salinity in the 
Murray-Darling river system is a case in point. Biotechnology may ` 
well be able to engineer a plant which will take up excess salt into 
its leaves. However, what is the cause of the salinity problem? 
Salinity is occurring due to the overuse of irrigation and extensive 
land clearing to make way for sheep and cattle grazing and 


monoculture cropping systems. This highly inefficient form ОЁ, / 


agriculture should not be propped up by a salt-harvesting plant but 
scrapped and replaced with diverse agricultural systems based on the. 
cycles of life.

wounded the woman selling flowers. Two homeless people also suffered from the blast. Obviously, the purpose 
of the bomb was not so much to kill but to terrorise. 


Usually 4.20 p.m. would be the moment when most people gather in the square for food. Luckily, that day the 
Food Not Bombs serving was happened later than usual and the activists showed up on Viadimirskaya a few 
minutes after the explosion. The food was given to the homeless without any problems.

between organised crime, for example. But when later that day it was revealed that bomb had a timing device, 
people considered that Food Not Bombs may have been targeted on purpose by neonazis. The use of explosives 
is nothing new for Russian nazis. In Moscow on December 22, 2006 a bomb set up against local antifascist

Sovereignty Day - 26th January. 
Aboriginal Tent Embassy, Canberra 


Every year there is a gathering in Canberra to bring together different groups and elders from around the country 
at the Aboriginal tent embassy. I was invited by an Aunty to stay for two weeks to help build it and prepare 
the site for when people started trickling in. Many walked and cycled from all different cities including from 
Sydney where a big bloc had formed of environmental activists united against uranium. 


On the day there was a positive peace march from the city back to the embassy. A sacred fire was carried around 
the city and back to the peace and justice fire at the embassy. We were welcomed by elder Aunty Isabel Coe 
with Uncle Speedy McGuinness from up north who also spoke some words about the demand of sovereignty 
now and the need to keep up the tradition of the oldest ongoing protest site in the world, the one place where it 
is 500 nations working together. 


After the ceremony, the kinds of stories I’d heard from Aboriginal people visiting the embassy for to weeks 
all came together in a powerful and moving barrage of speeches in the massive Chai tent. Uncle Speedy spoke 
about how looking after country is an issue for white and black Australia, especially the violent mining and 
dumping of uranium. The most moving speech was from a woman who had two of her boys taken by police and 
killed in custody for her own outspoken political stance in meetings with government. 


During the speeches we received the news that finally charges had been laid against the pig who had been found 
responsible by the coroner for one death in custody in Palm Island, two years after it happened. Spokespeople 
from the Queensland police union that day threatened to strike in protest of the charges being laid despite the 
riots that had occurred there that week for another death in custody. 


Invasion day kicked off talks that went for another two weeks, including meetings to decide how to fight the 
push from government-paid Ngunawal elders (from the area around Canberra) who want to turn the site into a 
Canberra-esque education centre instead of a protest site, ending 35 years of grass-roots history. But this won’t 
happen of course because people know it always was and always will be, Aboriginal land.

FREE AS IN FREEDOA, NOT FREE AS IN BEER 


by Changie McChangemind 


he term intellectual property (IP) is one that may be familiar, especially to students and 

anyone that works for a corporation. Its general meaning is fairly clear: an idea or concept 
that someone produces can be legally treated is if it were a tangible object. This means, for 
example, that you could take someone to court for stealing it, or using it without permission. 


Common examples of IP are: 
* Copyright * Trademarks »* Patents 


Common uses of IP outside the formal structures also exist. 


University students doing research projects often have their work declared intellectual 
property of either the university they attend or of the professor they work under. This differs 
widely from campus to campus, and in each individual situation. Many universities have IP 
policies which are based on or comply with the National Principles of Intellectual Property 
Management for Publicly Funded Research (http://www.nhmre.gov.au/funding/policy/ 
ipmanage.htm). This is perhaps not particularly insidious by itself, however it can raise 
problems with students who genuinely want to work towards a furtherment of their field of 
study to benefit the wider community, especially considering the close ties of universities, 
defence companies and departments, and ethics free industries. 


Similarly, corporate workers often have to sign over IP to anything they work on, for example 
if they automate a process in their work using a spreadsheet, the spreadsheet design and macros 
become property of that company. 


It is interesting from an activist’s point.of view to compare this to Pierre-Joseph Proudhon’s 
theories about the two different components of the common definition of property: property 
which is used to further tyranny and property which is used to protect liberty. 


Proponents of IP state that it falls in to the 
latter category, or at least doesn’t fall in to 

the first category. The general claim is that 

IP helps develop ideas and concepts by 

providing the IP owner an exclusive right to 
exploit the fruits of their intellectual labours. 
Presumably, this is an argument that the IP 

owner’s economic interests are represented by the IP 

and thus the development of the IP will be well supported because 
the owner wants to receive the highest possible economic benefit. 
Once the term of IP ownership is expired, the concept in question 
would get released in to the public domain, thus for the benefit of 
society as a whole. 


There are several criticisms of this system and they are fairly easy 
to find on the internet, so I’ll keep my summary of them brief. 


Keeping exclusive ownership of ideas is by definition keeping that idea out of the public 
domain and thus use of that idea becomes un-democratic, in the sense that no-one else has 
any say in how it is developed. In an environment where private companies and projects are 
routinely scrapped as tax write-offs, it is dubious whether an idea being in the private domain 
offers it more protection against loss that if it had no legal ownership at all. 


The concept itself is mostly negative: that is, the direct advantage of having something 
declared one’s IP is that any non-authorised party using IP can be sued or otherwise stopped 
from using it. This is direct contrast with the supposed benefits to the public domain mentioned 
above. 


An owner of IP is also free to withhold the IP from public use until a time more suitable to that 
owner, for example when there will be more market demand for it. As there is no guarantee 
that the IP holder will behave in an ethical manner, this can raise serious concerns for 
community welfare. 


A good example of the implementation of IP copyright is on the documentary The Corporation, 
when they describe that the song Happy Birthday is the property of and AOL/Time Warner 
subsidiary, and that they. would have to pay a fee to play it. 


Although IP can mean different things in different parts of the world, efforts are being made 
by the World Trade Organization (WTO) through the Agreement on Trade Related Aspects 

of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS) to enforce unified IP guidelines. Being a creation 

of the WTO, it is characterized by provisions that set up wealth flow from “developing” to 
“developed” nations, as a result of heavy lobbying from the US, Europe, and Japan. It is based 
on The Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works and the Paris 
Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property and is enforceable by the WTO’s Dispute 
Settlement Body (DSB), an organisation that can “advise” members to take action to bring its 
laws, regulations or policies into conformity with the WTO Agreements. 


Anyway, so doom and gloom, what interests me about IP is that several groups and individuals 
are working on ways to keep information free (‘free as in freedom, not free as in beer’, as 
famously declared by the GNU project). I’ve listed three below: 


GNU General Public Licence (GPL) 


Probably the most commonly encountered among computer users, the GPL is 
a free software licence, developed by Richard Stallman for the GNU project. 


Basically, the GPL requires that any user of software has access to the original 
source code of that software, for non-nerds: the program before it has been compiled (made 
into a program), and as such it’s easy to edit and change by a user with the will and know-how, 


The user is then free to distribute any modified version, or even sell them, but MUST include 
references to the original program, and again, the source code. 


Also, the redistribution must also be licensed under GPL. 


On September 6, 2006, the gpl-violations.org project prevailed in court litigation against D- 
Link Germany GmbH regarding D-Link’s alleged inappropriate and copyright infringing use 
of parts of the Linux Operating System Kernel. The judgement finally provided the on-record, 


legal precedent that the GPL is valid and legally binding, and that it will stand up in German 
court. 


This is a very rough overview, please. read up if interested! 
Copyleft 


A copyleft is a type of license, obviously a play on words on copyright. A 

copyleft license uses copyright law to ensure that every person who receives a 

copy of a work has the same rights to study, use, modify, and also redistribute both the work, 
and derived versions of the work. 


The GPL mentioned above is a type of copyleft license. The license originated with Richard 
Stallman, founder of the GNU project, when he was refused access to an improved version of a 
program he had originally provided a company under a public domain agreement.

an attempt to bring Palestinian stories to 

a larger audience. As such, whilst the 
documentary centres on her efforts and

Jenin refugee camp, it is the Palestinians

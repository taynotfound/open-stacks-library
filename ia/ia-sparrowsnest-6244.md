THE BACKGROUND 

In July 1979, after 16 years of mounting popular 
resistance and guerilla warfare, the dictatorship of 
Anastasia Somoza was overthrown by an armed insurrect¬ 
ion of the Nicaraguan people led by the FSLN (the 
Sandinista Front for national Liberation ) 

For Nicaragua it was far more than the replacement 
of one government with smother. Effectively it was 
the birth of sin independent and authentic Nicaragua 
- a Nicaragua ruled not by Spain or later the USA 
and its local henchmen but rather by and for the . 
workers and peasants of the country. It was also 
the birth of a most unusual experiment in "socialism" 
and so far a remarkably succesful one. 

Despite the poverty stricken and war ruined state of 
the country in 1979, despite 6 years of continuous 
war against the Contras (ex National Guardsmen from 
Somoza*s days and mercenaries backed by the USA), 
despite an economic blockade by the USA and the 
problems common to all Lati*n America of crippling 
debt burdens and deteriorating terms of trade with 
the developed world. Despite all this the 3 million 
Nicaraguans have made headway in the economic and 
cultural development of their country, there is a 
more equal sharing out of what little wealth there 
is, and the FSLN government remains very popular. 

In the 1984 elections, the first unrigged elections 
in Nicaragua’s history, the FSLN took % of the vote 
on a 75 % turnout (though the FSLN would be at pains 
to point out that 'elections* are only one small part 
of the process of exerting democratic control). And 
whilst I was visiting Nicaragua in July the Rally 
on Independence day attracted 4/500,000 people which 
is 15 % of the country. Imagine a demonstration in 
London of 8 million people', for thats the equivalent. 



I came back from Nicaragua feeling inspired by what 
is happening there and immensely impressed by the 
Sandinistas as a political force. I think the 'left' 
in Britain has an awful lot to learn from them - 
about what a democratic socialist society could be 
like, about how to involve people in controlling that 
society, and about strategy and tactics. For when you 
are trying to take on poverty, a local dictator, US 
imperialism, the world economy, patriarchy - and there 
is 3 million of you; then you have to take decisions 
about what to do first, who to combat and with whom 
to make temporary alliances, with the price for 
getting it wrong often being death 

It was also so refreshing to see a 'left' party that 
is not stuck in the rights and wrongs of the Russian 
revolution, that feels confident in taking bits out 
of Marx and Lenin, combining it with Latin American 
nationalism, Catholic Liberation Theology and a lot 
of original thought, applying it to the particular 
reality of Nicaragua now, and being prepared to 
freely and openly admit when they've made mistakes. 

I could talk and write for hours about Nicaragua - 
but here I'll just try to give a flavour of the place 
with a few examples. 



1) THE STATE/ PRIVATE ENTERPRISE AND COOPERATIVES 

- LAND REFORM 

In 1979 all land was privately owned, with most of 
it including all the best land, in the hands of a 
small number of large landowners. A story common to 
much of the world. 6 years on 16$ is state owned 
and nearly 40 % in cooperatives, and whilst the rest 
is still in private hands there has been some redist¬ 
ribution of that from large to small peasant farmers. 
Progress certainly but far from the conventional 
nationalise everything approach of many 'socialist' 
countries. The FSLN strategy for land reform has 
followed the following principles;- 

- To have taken over all private property would have 
turned a large number of peasant farmers against- the 
revolution at atime when, under attack from outside, 
the last thing it needed was an internal war. 

It would also have turned into opponents a lot of 
people with considerable agricultural expertise and, 
as has often happened in 'Socialist' countries, 
productivity would have initially fallen drastically 
leading to even greater poverty. Much land has been 
expropriated, that of Somoza and his cronies, of 
private owners who weren't farming it properly or 
deliberately de-capitalising it; and some has been 
purchased by agreement or compulsorily. BUT private 
farming as a principle hasn't been attacked. As a

The visitor to Nicaragua can't help but be struck by 
how militarised the place is. Not in terms of heavy 
armour, but in terms of how many young people there 
are in military uniforms and how many people have guns. 
It is the mark of a country that had to fight to oust 
a brutal dictatorship, has been under attack ever 
since, and is preparing for the realistic possibility 
of a direct US invasion. Yet it is also, out of the 
war zones, the most relaxed, friendly and safest 
country I have ever been in. A 13 year old Nicaraguan 
child carrying a rifle, as some do, may not have had 
the advanced weapons training of a Brixton policeman 
but his/her awareness of what guns are for, when to 
use them and when not to use them, is considerably 
higher. 

And when the police stop you in the street and ask for 
identification you don't feel the normal threats 
associated with 'authority'. Slather one feels one 
is talking to a comrade and equal, part of whose role 
is to remorselessly hunt the efforts of the CIA (and 
they are numerous) to commit economic sabotage and 
political assasination, The police and the army are 
trained to fight and kill but they are also 'educated' 
in its deepest sense to be humble and to be servants 
of the people. This is where artistic sensitivity 
comes in. Abuses of power and authority occur, the 
Nicaraguans have not become saints overnight, but 
these are treated as serious crimes in terms of 
punishment. 

Of Nicaragua's 3 million people half are under 15. 
200,000 adults (and some children) have arms and have 
been trained in their use. The majority are not in 
the armed forces or the police but rather form 
voluntary militias to fight off Contra guerilla attacks 
or the Americans if they come. Not an army but a 
people armed, and.an indication of the confidence 
the Sandinistas have in the relationship between 
themselves and the people. Imagine Thatcher or Reagan 
freely handing ou£ arms to workers ? 

3 ) LEARNING FROM MISTAKES ~ THE MISK1T0 PROBLEM 

Nicaragua is 95$ Mestizo - mixed Spanish/Indian blood. 
But on the remote Atlantic coast region there are 
about 100,000 indigenous Indians, mainly Miskitos, 
and 100,000 Creoles who are English speaking Blacks 
brought there originally as slaves to work the sugar 
plantations. These ethnic groups, particularly the 
Indians, have been subject as everywhere else in 
the American continent, to centuries of systematic 
racism and exploitation. The Sandinistas of 1979 
consciously espoused a non-racist policy. Yet in 
their early practice out of ignorance and conditioning 
much of what they did could be labelled as institu¬ 
tional racism. The Miskitos and Creoles were taught to ] 
read and write Spanish like it or not, and generally 
no account was taken of specific cultural needs. 

Army abuses did occur and conflicts quickly arose 
with Contra propoganda encouraging many Miskitos to 
leave the country; some to take up arms but many to 
end up in far worse conditions in Honduras. This 
issue has perhaps been the revolution's biggest test 
of its honesty and ability to be self-critical. 

In a few years there has been a major turn-around. 
Education now in the atlantic coast is bi-lingual; 
economic resources are being put into the area to 
compensate for its traditional under-development; 
and a mass consultative process is under way to 
devise a constitution for a regional government 
with considerable powers of economic self government 
and autonomous cultural development. Devising such a 
constitution is not easy for whilst the Miskitos 
rightly wish for less domination by the rest of 
Nicaragua, the smaller Indian tribes are just as 
worried about cultural domination by the Miskitos. 

And whilst the indigenous Indians have a right to 
greater autonomy one needs to avoid over-romanticising 
them as a people for some have been happy to throw 
in their lot with Reagan and the CIA. 

When the Minister of the Interior announced the 
Autonomy proposals to a mass rally of Miskitos he 
delivered his entire speech in Miskito using not just 
the words but the cultural idioms too... Margaret 
Thatcher in Welsh or Punjabi ? 



4) THE NEW NATION,..THE NEW MAN (.SIC) 

An independent but pro-Sandinista daily newspaper 
that began in 1980 carries the logo -'the new paper 
for the new man'. The concept of the new man has 
been central to the Sandinistas political project 
from their beginnings in 1963. The new man is selfles 
rather than egotistic, collective rather than 
individualistic, intensely patriotic yet with a 
strong internationalist consciousness, in particular 
an awareness of the need for unity amongst the Latin 
American peoples. 

In its origins the concept owes nothing to Western 
feminism, but in its practice it is clearly question¬ 
ing many of the manifestations of machismo. Selfless¬ 
ness and collectivity forged in a long guerilla war 
where most of the young men and women who formed the 
guerilla army in the early years died in the process 
whilst others suffered imprisonment and torture. 

Nicaragua is full of reminders of dead heroes, some 
of them 'leaders', most lesser known fighers. Picture 
on the walls; memorials in the neighbourhoods; bus 
stations, shopping centres and estates named after 
them. The dead leaders are given more emphasis than 
the living ones. A new nation creates its historical 
identity. 

AND THE NEW WOMAN 

It may not be called feminism but the development of 
the power of women has been considerable. By their 
involvment in the guerilla struggle young women 
pushed themselves onto the political scene and they 
haven't renounced it since. The armed forces retain 
large numbers of women (though they have tended to 
be kept out of the front line), the neighbourhood 
defence committees which are also the effective 
community based organisations, are predominantly 
women, and progress is being made in terms of women 
entering traditional male jobs. Women have a number 
of places in the 2nd tier of government posts, though 
not yet at the top, and AMNLAE, the Nicaraguan Womens 
Association, whilst not an autonomous movement in the 
Western sense since it is part of the Sandinista part 
is not backward in raising womens issues within the 
party, government and trade unions. 

The government attitude is supportive, instituting 
changes on the economic level to do with equal pay, 
job discrimination, rights within the family; and 
very effectively banning the use of womens bodies » 
for advertising purposes. But also cautious, avoiding 
too direct a confrontation with male power. Perhaps 
too cautious but aware, as in all its policies of the 
need to maintain national unity against the all too in 
threat from imperialism. 


Women are on the move in Nicaragua, and way in 
advance of developments anywhere else in Latin 
America. A long way still to go, but as elsewhere 
in the world women are changing roles faster then men

CLASSIC’S CORNER 


ANNE BRONTE 
The Tenant Of 
Wildfell Hall. 



Born a vicar's daughter in the 1830's,sent 
to an austere and reactionary school for the 
education of clergymen's children, it never 
ceases to amaze me that Anne Bronte could have 
produced what is to me the first feminist novel 
Her heroine, Helen Huntingdon, flees a 
drunken and violent husband and taking her child 
with her, causes curiosity when she takes up 
residence in a false name in a little known part 
of the country. She maintains her independance 
not only physically and financially, but also 
in thought, standing her ground against the 
opinions of her neighbours. 

But yet again I hear the Anarchist whinings 
'it's all about religion(yuk!)' And oh, horror 
of horror, she actually gets married in the endI 
Well if we calm ourselves a little and look 
at the novel and the author in the context of 
the times, what we read is .almost revolutionary. 
The very fact of a woman leaving her husband 
scandalised all social conventions, women and 
children were men's property under law and Helen 
Huntingdon's act was illegal. Society at the tim 
time was even more worried about a woman writing 
about such things. 

Through Helen Huntingdon we hear the voice 
of Anne Bronte declaring to the world the 
equality of men and women, the fallibility of 
men and the strength of will and perseverance 
that women can possess.Her eventual marriage 
and acceptance by the community is a tool used 
by Bronte, an illustration of hope, that humanity 
can change, acceptance is given. 

So give Marge Piercy and Emma Goldman a 
rest for a bit and read the ones who made it 
possible. Open your minds and hear a cry from 
a bleak northern village, maybe it'll touch your 
heart.

Flyposting 


A recent decision in the Court of 
Appeal has reinforced advice which 
Head Office has given parties for 
many years. 

Posters advertising the British 
Union for the Abolition of 
Vivisection were flyposted on empty 
shops in Derby. 

Derby Council prosecuted BUAV under 
the Town & Country Planning Act 1971 
which allows a prosecution against 
the person or body benefiting from 
the advertisements, as well as the 
actual person sticking up the 
posters. Derby magistrates accepted 
BUAV * s submission that they did not 
authorise the illegal bill sticking, 
had no knowledge of it, and did not 
agree with it. 

Derby Council appealed, pointing 
out that they had written to BUAV'S 
head office, ordering them to remove 
the posters. BUAV's failure to do 
so made them liable to conviction, 
because flyposting was a 
'continuing* offence in law. 

Mr Justice Mann said there were 
good policy reasons why the offence 
should be construed as 'continuing'. 
It gives the local planning 


authority an opportunity to secure 
the removal of flyposted bills by 
those whose business Is advertised 
rather than at the expense of the 
ratepayer. The Court of Appeal 
ordered Derby magistrates to 
reconsider the case.

HISTORY IS A DRiw. 

History is a number of other things too, but 
history is a dru§, addictive in most cases. 
Physical damage is limited except for the odd 
case of scholar's silicosis caused by too much 
blowing dust off ancient books. 

The real damage is intellectual. The subject 
would rather live in the past than change the 
present. The symtoms are obvious* the retreat 
into worab-like book lined rooms; the necro¬ 
philiac delight in skeletons in cupboards; the 
preference for the finished perfection of the 
dead and gone. 

1 know all this because I've done many lines 
of history. When I started to write my book on 
the British Anarchist Movement in 1974 it was 
already obvious that the high tide from student 
revolution (I 968 ) to miner's strike ( 1972 } had 
slipped back and'the institutions had re-estab¬ 
lished themselves. Everyone had explanations 
but nobody knew why. 

So f I retreated from the area of agitation to 
the area of cogitation. I went back to my poli¬ 
tical roots and made a new world for myself. 

But understand* I didn't invent a damn thing. 
Every statement of fact has a source. Every 
opinion of mine of these facts is clearly stated 
to be an opinion. But I chose what was important. 
I still believe my choices to be right. But it is 
my version. 

The "real" world of the time may be guessed at 
from an^ reading of the newspapers, of the 
magazines, the books, the letters, the accounts 
of trials that survive the period. On the same 
page as an anarchist trial are accounts of 
strikes, suicides and society divorces. Gold is 
discovered in one place, some missionary disapp¬ 
ears in another.

Once at Lambley, we busied, ouroelves with setting up camp 
and making a fire. The area around our /ield was a bit like 
an SWP meeting - there was lots of dead wood around. It was 
already late and dark by the time we*d got ourselves cosy, so 
the younger ©dventurers soon went to bed whilst the older ones 
set about the alcohol. Conversation around the campfire 
gradually became less relevant to the Glorious Peoples* Strugg. 
and the first of the weekend’s amazing vomits took place short! 
thereafter* __ 


The next day, Saturday, saw no anarchists lined up in patrols 
to have their shoes inspected. Some of us had a refreshing 
wash down in the exhilaratingly freezing stream, but most relied 
on the pub at lunchtime to wake them up properly, (it didn’t 
seem to work too well for the people who’d been up all night, 
though.) 


The afternoon was spent on the swing, (which we’d hung from 
a tree, and which didn’t snap until next day), playing games 
which involved hurling ourselves at each other and rolling over 
each other, and, of course, vomitting. 


The author of this report is not too clear as to what else 
happened on Saturday, due to the stupor induced at lunchtime, 
which soon turned into a blinding head ache, but would like to 


commend the collectively produced meal. This gastronomic 
delight was mutually created by everyone throwing whatever they 
had into the pot, from garlic to celery to homebrew. There was 
tons of it, and its splendour has been immortalised in a 
specially composed song: "Potato , Rice and Carrot Stew". 


Sunday, it rained. We got wet. We packed up, cleared off, 
and caught a bus back to town, where one happy camper nearly 
knocked himself unconscious falling off the bus. 

So, the moral of the story is - why not become an anarchist, 
and you, too, could drink yourself silly in congenial company 
in a wet countryside. It’s good fun! (Non-alcoholics also 

welcome.)

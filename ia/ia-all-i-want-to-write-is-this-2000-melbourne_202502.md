that shop looks at me and Knows why | want that balaclava. | feel like those 
few words on the radio add up to the clarity | have been looking for: those 
words say more than anyone or anything about what's what. | feel like 
certain facts about how the world is run and in whose interests are now 
hard-wired into my nervous system. There is a thin grey film separating me 
from the world, a layer of lies and banalities | am now viscerally, physically 
apart from, and the concrete and power lines and shopfronts retreat into 
the background as the force of the world comes out to meet me. This is the 
imaginary world, the one we're tricked into living in, this blur of fake 
sameness. The real world is what happens when you punch a hole in the 
fake one, and you luxuriate in the openness of the space you've created. 
Sex and drugs and dancing and new ideas and love and revolution. Those 
moments and hours and days we steal from the dead time we live in are 
(for me) the only things that make life worth living but almost impossible to 


talk about because our language is so cheap and corrupt. You know what | 
mean. 


So | head home trying not to burst into tears, thinking, at least wait untif you 
get inside. And when | do get back to Leah’s and find nobody home | just 
curl up fetally on the couch and cry like some drug-crazed soap star, 
Anger, guilt, remorse. Finally, making myself calm, | think: Okay. The thing 
| was most afraid of has happened. Right now | need to regroup but 
tomorrow I'll be ready. | think. It suddenly occurs to me the forever I've 
been awake is really only four hours and | have a little burst of hysterical 
laughter trying to reconcile those facts. 


| spend the day sweeping the channels for any news report which of course 
| know will be absolute bullshit but what else can | do? | get more and more 
worried about Az and the others and spend hours sobbing hysterically on 
and off and eventually go to sleep for a while. When | wake up | feel much 
more together, like I've now had an amount of sleep that vaguely 
resembles what | actually need. When | turn on the 5 o'clock news and see 
that they're expecting more trouble as the police try to get the delegates out 
at seven, I’m out the door and back to Crown. 


I'm still afraid |’m not strong enough to do this. But if now isn't the time to 
get back on the horse, | don’t know when will be. 


So | spend over an hour in a blockade face to face with rows of pigs with 
batons out and no name badges, riot cops, attack dogs, horses, 
motorbikes, etc. It’s a huge calculated effort to scare us and make us back 
down but we hold that line. The boring trot chants turn into unselfconscious 
pleas for the police not to hurt us. “We're nonviolent, what about you?” “The 
world is watching. Your mum is watching.” “Please don't hurt us. Please 
don't hurt us. Please don't hurt us.” The cops don't appear to have any 
emotional response to this whatsoever and maintain their intimidating 
inhuman facelessness. Stupidly, improbably, | am actually not scared. | 
think {'ve spent so much of the last 24 hours in a state of abject fear and 
terror I've burnt out those emotions and can only stay calm in the 
expectation that at any moment those pigs will start attacking us. I'm ready 
for it and will do whatever | can to help my uh comrades. In fact, right now, 
| could almost use the word “comrades” without embarrassment or irony. 


But they don't attack us. They break one of the other gates, targeting the 
young men in the blockade so as to break our morale, on the (untrue, 
deeply stupid) assumption that young men are the strongest and toughest 
of us; they grab peoples’ heads and slam them against the concrete. None 
of them are wearing their,ID badges. Don't anybody try to tell me pigs 
aren't despicable. 


But... | feel sorta bad about this but | feel amazing. | don’t think I’m being 
too presumptuous if | say, we feel amazing. That's what S11 is about, to 
me. We find a way to tear through the dishonest screens between us and 
the world and we somehow manage to hold that tear open for three days, 
even though many of us are falling apart, falling apart partly because we're 
touching this intensity that can’t survive the world we live in. And all this 
inseparable from the risks we are taking. There is a certain crazy equation 
we are in the process of formulating which revolves around terms like risk, 
identity, joy and revolution: impossible, yes, but wonderful just the same. Or 
wonderful because impossible. Or wonderful equals impossible. A mad 
audacity that carries you through into kinds of creativity and excitement you 
can't describe. Or at least that’s how it is for me. 


S11 is over. Yesterday | felt like | wouldn't be able to talk to people who 
weren't there because there was such an incredible disjunction between our 
lives, I'd wander round like a crazed Vietnam vet, ranting, “You wouldn't 
know BECAUSE YOU WEREN'T THERE.” Today | can feel myself going 
back towards whatever we call “normality”. And | need that, but | don’t think 
| want it. I've spent three days hopped up on adrenaline, sleep deprivation 
and solidarity, feeling some of the most intense emotions of my life: | don’t 
want to have to go back to the real world. 


| find myself bursting into tears at those moments which seem to separate 
re from the last few days. When | pass Crown on the train and see the 
traffic passing casually through those streets we fought so hard to hold, I’m 
overwhelmed with depression and panic. The same thing the next day 
when | empty out my backpack, loaded with blockading supplies and 
revolutionary paraphernalia, to fill it with the things | need for school. On the 
bus | get totally panicked by the crowd and unable to relate to the way 
we're occupying space, dense but not defensive. It doesn’t seem right. 


A week later |'m doing fine; S11 is just memories now. Then | realise that’s 
bullshit. | take a break from working on this article to go get tea and while 
I'm sitting in the shop | realise | am absolutely fucking terrified, can't stand 
to be alone, desperately need other people around me if I’m going to fee! 
okay. Driving home | try not to cry and when | get inside my dinner goes 
cold waiting for me to pull myself together. | wish | hadn't given all my 
stuffed toys to charity years ago; | could use Panda right about now. | fee! 
hysterically lonely and miserable. It isn't fair. None of it's fucking fair. The 
only thing that relieves the feeling of despair is to try to channel it into anger 
or at least distract myself by working on the zine, working out my sadness 
in words. As long as | can keep typing run-on sentences, mad 
ungrammatical rants, schizoid pornography, | can keep my distance from 
the emotional shitheap I’m so close to collapsing in to. Maybe that explains 
why this story is so fucking long. 


| start having nightmares, one after the other all through the night. | don't 
remember many of them except the general unbearable sense of 
impending doom that runs through them. !n one I'm blockading a small 
room inside Crown with a dozen or so senior citizens when the cops bring 
out some scary thing called a bootsuit to attack us with: people are badly 
hurt and everything collapses into a blur of absurd acts of violence. In 
another |'m trying to read aloud a very, very important passage from a book 
but somebody, | think maybe Eve, tells me nobody can understand what 
I'm saying. | realise my tongue is falling apart inside my mouth. My vocal 
cords are disintegrating. | don’t know how to make anybody understand the 
things | need to say. 


| feel guilty about the trouble I’m having coping because | didn't get hurt: 
like | don't have the right to feel bad because others copped so much 
worse. | know that’s stupid. On the other hand, I'm proud of it all because 
these are my (our) battle scars; the tears and nightmares are the markers 
of something we did that was fucking amazing and heroic and powerful. We 
stood up to the cops, to the government, to the most powerful institutions in 
the world in the face of exhaustion and cold and rain and misery and in 
spite of all that, we found our victories and exhilarations. We danced. | 
wouldn't swap it for anything. | just wish | could make us all okay again.
